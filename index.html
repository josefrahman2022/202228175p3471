Inflation is falling – so why does life still feel so expensive?

Data on wages, rents and household bills suggest that for many UK households, financial pressure remains high despite slowing inflation.

In recent months, headlines across the UK have pointed to falling inflation as evidence that the worst of the cost-of-living crisis may finally be easing.Official figures from the Office for National Statistics show that the rate of price increases has slowed significantly compared with the sharp rises seen in the years following the pandemic. On paper, this sounds like positive news.

For many households, however, everyday life still feels far from cheaper. Weekly supermarkets shops remain noticeably more expensive than they were just a few years ago, private rents continue to rise, and household bills still place significant pressure on monthly budgets. For many people, the economic recovery described in the headlines does not seem to match what they experience in their day-to-day lives.

This contradiction lies at the heart of the UK’s current economic reality. Falling inflation does not necessarily mean that prices are falling. Instead, it means that prices are still rising, but at a slower pace than before. By looking at data on wages, rents and essential housing costs, this story explores why financial pressure remains high for many households despite improving headline inflation figures.

Why falling inflation does not mean falling prices

Despite the positive tone that often comes with recent inflation announcements, the term itself is frequently misunderstood.A fall in inflation doesn’t mean that prices are going down. Instead, it means that the rate at which prices are increasing has slowed. For example, if inflation falls from 8% to 3%, the cost of goods and services is still rising, just at a slower pace than before. prices therefore continue to increase rather than returning to pre-crisis levels.

This distinction is central to understanding why many households continue to feel under financial strain. While the headline inflation rate may suggest that economic conditions are improving, the cumulative effect of several years of sustained price increases means that the cost of essentials remains significantly higher than it was before the pandemic. Items such as groceries, household energy and transport costs may no longer be rising as sharply as they were during the peak of the crisis, but they have not become cheaper in absolute terms.

For many consumers, what matters is not the rate of change itself, but the actual amount being spent each week or month. A household that has had to adjust to paying more for food, rent and utility bills over the past few years may still feel very little sense of relief, even if the official inflation rate has fallen. This helps explain why public perception often appears disconnected form the economic headlines and why many people continue to feel that the cost-of-living crisis is far from over.

Essential costs remain high

For many households, the continuing sense of financial pressure is closely linked to the cost of essential spending rather than headline economic indicators alone. Food prices, for example, remain significantly higher than were before the sharp rise in inflation between 2021 and 2023. Although the annual rate of food inflation has slowed form its peak, the overall cost of a weekly supermarket shop has not returned to earlier levels. Recent data shows that food prices rose by around 25% between January 2022 and January 2024, highlighting the cumulative effect of sustained price increases over time.

Housing pressures continue to outweigh relief

Housing costs have continued to place further strain on household budgets.Private rents across the UK have remained on an upward trajectory, with recent ONS figures showing continued year-on-year growth in average monthly rents. Even when rental inflation has begun to slow, households are still paying substantially more than in previous years, meaning that any sense of relief from falling inflation figures may feel limited in practice

The data also shows that housing costs have continued to place significant pressure on household budgets. Private rent growth rose 1.1% in 2021 to 8.7% in 2025, far outpacing the fall in headline inflation during the same period. This sharp increase helps explain why many households, particularly renters, continue to feel financially stretched even as official inflation figures improve.

Energy costs also continue to shape public perceptions of affordability. While Ofgem’s price cap has eased compared with the peaks seen during the energy crisis, household bills remain high relative to pre-pandemic norms, particularly during colder months when usage increases. For many households this means that even if inflation is slowing on paper monthly outgoings still feel significantly higher than they did a few years ago.

Wages have not fully restored living standards

A key reason why many households continue to feel financially stretched is that wage growth has not always translated into meaningful improvements in living standards.While average earnings in great Britain have continued to rise over the long term, the pace of wage growth has recently slowed. According to the lates figures from the Office for National Statistics, average regular pay growth was 3.8% in three months to January 2026, down from 4.2% in the previous period.

Recent labour market data also suggest that real pay growth has weakened. While nominal wages have increased, inflation-adjusted earnings have grown only marginally, meaning that any improvement in purchasing power has been limited. For many workers, particularly in the private sector where wage growth has slowed to around 3.3%, this has translated into a continued sense of financial pressure rather than genuine financial relief.

This gap between wage growth and lived affordability is particularly visible when compared with housing costs. Private rents across many parts of the UK have continued to rise a pace that outstrips wage increases, meaning that a larger share of monthly income is still being directed towards housing. As a result, even improving headline economic indicators may offer little comfort to households whose disposable income remains under strain.

While wage growth did recover 5.2% in 2022 to 6.1 % in 2023, the sharp rise in rent growth to 8.5 in 2024 and 8.7 % in 2025 suggests that housing costs continued to absorb a growing share of household income. This further reinforces why many households still do not feel that economic conditions have genuinely improved.

The recovery is visible in the data, but not always in daily life

Recent economic headlines suggest that the worst of the inflation crisis may be beginning to ease. The annual inflation rate has fallen from the highs reached in previous years, and wage growth, while slowing, continues to rise modestly. Yet for many households across the UK, the lived reality remains far more complicated. Regular pay growth has slowed to 3.8% while private sector wage growth has eased further to 3.3%, limiting any meaningful improvement in real household spending power.

At the same time, many of costs that dominate household budgets remain elevated. Private rents contuse to rise, with average UK rents increasing by 3.5% year-on-year, while food and energy bills remain significantly above pre-pandemic levels. The result is a widening disconnect between economic headlines and everyday experience. While the pace of price increases may have slowed, prices themselves remain high, and the cumulative effect of several years of inflation continues to shape household finances.

This helps explain why many people still feel that life has not become cheaper despite improving headline figures. Falling inflations is, in statistical terms a sign of economic improvement. But for households facing rising rents, persistently high supermarket bills and limited real wage growth, financial relief remains difficult to felt in practice. The recovery may be visible in the data, but for many households it has yet to be fully reflected in daily life. By 2025, inflation had fallen to 3.6%, yet rent growth remained at 8.7% reinforcing the disconnect between economic headlines and everyday affordability.
