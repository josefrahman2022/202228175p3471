Two data-driven feature stories examining the UK cost-of-living crisis, inflation, housing pressure and uneven economic recovery.

Inflation is falling – so why does life still feel so expensive?

Data on wages, rents and household bills suggest that for many UK households, financial pressure remains high despite slowing inflation.

Hook

The headline suggests recovery

In recent months, headlines across the UK have pointed to falling inflation as evidence that the worst of the cost-of-living crisis may finally be easing.

Official figures from the Office for National Statistics show that the rate of price increases has slowed significantly compared with the sharp rises seen in the years following the pandemic.

For many households, however, everyday life still feels far from cheaper.

Why falling inflation does not mean falling prices

Despite the positive tone that often comes with recent inflation announcements, the term itself is frequently misunderstood.

A fall in inflation does not mean that prices are going down. Instead, it means that prices are still rising, but at a slower pace.

Essential costs remain high

For many households, the continuing sense of financial pressure is closely linked to the cost of essential spending rather than headline economic indicators alone.

Housing pressures continue to outweigh relief

Housing costs have continued to place further strain on household budgets.

Wages have not fully restored living standards

A key reason why many households continue to feel financially stretched is that wage growth has not always translated into meaningful improvements in living standards.

The recovery is visible in the data, but not always in daily life

The recovery may be visible in the data, but for many households it has yet to be fully reflected in daily life.
